Antarctica

Continent Type: Icy, frozen desert
Area: Approximately 14 million square kilometers (5.4 million square miles)
Location: Located at the southernmost part of the Earth, surrounding the South Pole.
Key Facts about Antarctica:

View Map


Climate:

Antarctica is the coldest, driest, and windiest continent. Temperatures can drop as low as -80°C (-112°F) in winter and rarely rise above 0°C (32°F) even in summer. It is considered a desert because of its low precipitation—most of it is covered by ice that is up to 4.8 kilometers (3 miles) thick.

View Image


Wildlife:

Despite the harsh conditions, Antarctica is home to various species adapted to the cold, including penguins (like the Emperor and Adélie penguins), seals (such as the Weddell seal and leopard seal), and krill (small crustaceans). No terrestrial mammals or reptiles are native to Antarctica, and it has no indigenous human population.

View Imagep


Ice and Geography:

Around 98% of Antarctica is covered by ice, and the continent holds around 60-70% of the world's freshwater supply. It has the South Pole at its center, and the landmass is surrounded by the Southern Ocean. The Antarctic Peninsula is the most northern part of the continent, with some areas experiencing more temperate conditions.

View Image


Research:

Antarctica is a hub for scientific research, with more than 30 countries operating research stations across the continent. Major research topics include climate change, glaciology, biology, and astronomy. The Antarctic Treaty, signed in 1959, establishes Antarctica as a zone of international cooperation for scientific exploration and prohibits military activity or any permanent human settlement.

View Image


No Permanent Population:

There are no permanent residents in Antarctica. Instead, researchers and support staff live in temporary research stations, with populations fluctuating between about 1,000 in winter and over 5,000 in summer.

View Image


Environmental Protection:

Antarctica is protected by international agreements like the Antarctic Treaty System and Protocol on Environmental Protection to the Antarctic Treaty, which prohibits any exploitation of its natural resources (such as mining) and protects its unique environment.

View Image


Interesting Features:

Mount Vinson: The highest peak in Antarctica, reaching 4,892 meters (16,050 feet) above sea level.
Lake Vostok: A subglacial lake buried beneath 4 kilometers of ice, isolated from the outside world for millions of years.
Ross Ice Shelf: The largest ice shelf in Antarctica, it is a floating extension of the continent's ice sheet.
Antarctica remains one of the most unique and unexplored regions of the world, with its extreme conditions and fascinating ecosystems providing valuable scientific insights.
